# History of Computer Science: Early Computing

A comprehensive summary of computing history based on *Crash Course Computer Science #1*. This note covers the evolution of computing from hand-operated ancient tools to the electromechanical machines that laid the foundation for modern digital computers.

## 1. Ancient Foundations & The Need for Data Storage

As societies expanded, tracking trade, populations, and agriculture became too complex for the human mind alone. Humanity needed tools to offload mental labor.

*   **The Abacus (~2500 BC, Mesopotamia):** The earliest recognized computing device. A hand-operated tool using rows of beads representing powers of 10. 
    *   **Core Concepts:** It didn't just assist with addition and subtraction; it also **stored the current state** of the computation, functioning conceptually like a modern hard drive.
*   **Other Early Mathematical Tools:**
    *   *Astrolabe:* Allowed ships to calculate latitude at sea.
    *   *Slide Rule:* Assisted with rapid multiplication and division.
    *   *Mechanical Clocks:* Calculated sunrise, tides, and celestial movements.

## 2. When "Computer" Was a Job Title

Originally, a **computer** was not a machine; it was a professional human job title. 
*   **Origin (1613):** First recorded by author Richard Braithway to describe a person highly skilled at arithmetic and calculation.
*   **Human Computers:** For nearly three centuries, teams of human computers calculated mathematical tables (square roots, trigonometry, and military range tables for artillery) compiled into thick reference books.

---

## 3. The Shift to Mechanical Calculators

Manually computing tables was slow and prone to human error. Inventors sought to automate the process using mechanical gears.

*   **The Step Reckoner (1694):** Built by German polymath Gottfried Leibniz.
    *   **How it worked:** Used a system of 10-toothed gears representing digits 0–9. When a gear passed 9, it rotated back to 0 and advanced the adjacent gear by 1 (similar to a modern car odometer).
    *   **Significance:** It was the first machine capable of performing all four basic arithmetic operations: addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. Its core mechanism influenced calculator design for the next 300 years.

---

## 4. Charles Babbage & Ada Lovelace: The Dawn of Programmability

In the 19th century, English mathematician Charles Babbage recognized that specialized calculators were too limited. He designed systems that foreshadowed the modern computer architecture.

### The Difference Engine (1822)
*   A massive, 15-ton mechanical machine composed of 25,000 components designed to approximate complex polynomials, logarithmic functions, and trigonometric tables automatically.
*   Although Babbage never finished building it due to funding and manufacturing limits, historians built a fully functional version in 1991 using his original designs.

### The Analytical Engine (1830s)
*   **The First General-Purpose Computer:** Unlike previous single-purpose calculators, this machine was designed to run sequences of arbitrary operations.
*   **Key Architecture:** It featured an execution unit (like a CPU), internal memory, and a primitive printer.
*   **Ada Lovelace (The First Programmer):** Lovelace wrote hypothetical programs and instructions for the Analytical Engine. She realized that the machine could manipulate symbols beyond just pure numbers, creating the foundational language for computer programming.

## 5. Electromechanical Computing & The Birth of IBM

By the late 19th century, explosive population growth and globalization demanded faster data processing scales, particularly for government governance.

*   **The 1890 US Census Crisis:** The 1880 census took 7 full years to manually compile. With massive immigration, it was estimated that the 1890 census would take 13 years, making the data obsolete before it was finished.
*   **Herman Hollerith’s Tabulating Machine:** Hollerith built an electromechanical machine that automated data counting using **Punch Cards** (paper grids with specific hole configurations representing data points).
    *   **Mechanism:** Metal pins pressed down onto the cards. Wherever a hole was punched, the pin passed through into a vial of mercury, completing an electrical circuit that advanced a mechanical counting gear.
    *   **Impact:** The census was completed in just **2.5 years**, saving millions of dollars.
*   **The Foundation of IBM:** Recognizing the massive commercial potential for accounting, inventory, and business management, Hollerith founded the *Tabulating Machine Company*. In 1924, this company merged with others to become **IBM (International Business Machines Corporation)**.

## Key Terms Summary
| Concept / Machine | Inventor / Figure | Historical Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Abacus** | Ancient Mesopotamia | First device to calculate and store computational state. |
| **Computer** | Richard Braithway (1613) | Originally designated as a human job description for arithmetic professionals. |
| **Step Reckoner** | Gottfried Leibniz (1694) | First mechanical gear-based device to automate all 4 arithmetic operations. |
| **Analytical Engine** | Charles Babbage | The blueprint for the first general-purpose, programmable computer. |
| **First Programmer** | Ada Lovelace | Wrote the first symbolic algorithms/hypothetical code for the Analytical Engine. |
