## 1. Electromechanical Computing & Its Limits

In the early 20th century, rapid population growth and global conflict (World War I and II) required massive data processing. Early cabinet-sized devices expanded into room-sized electromechanical behemoths.

* **Harvard Mark I (1944):** Built by IBM for the Allies. It contained 765,000 components, 500 miles of wire, and was used to run simulations for the Manhattan Project.
* **The Core Mechanism — Relays:** Relays are electronically controlled mechanical switches. An electromagnetic coil pulls a metal arm shut to complete a circuit (acting like an electric water faucet).

![Harvard Mark I Computer](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Harvard_Mark_I_Processor.jpg/600px-Harvard_Mark_I_Processor.jpg)

### Why Relays Were Limited:
1. **Slow Speed:** Mechanical arms have mass. A relay could flick ~50 times per second. Complex math operations took seconds or minutes.
2. **Wear and Tear:** Moving mechanical parts degrade, stick, or break over time.
3. **Physical Bugs:** Heat and dark machinery attracted insects. In 1947, Grace Hopper's team pulled a dead moth from a faulty relay—coining the term **"computer bug"**.

## 2. Vacuum Tubes & The First Electronic Computers

To speed up computation, engineers replaced moving mechanical relays with **vacuum tubes** (thermionic valves), which have no moving parts.

* **How They Work:** Heating an electrode inside an airtight glass bulb releases electrons (thermionic emission). A third control electrode regulates whether electricity flows.
* **Advantages:** No mechanical wear and tear, and capable of switching thousands of times per second.
* **Disadvantages:** Fragile, can burn out like light bulbs, and initially expensive.

![Vacuum Tube Triode](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Triode_tube_diagram.svg/500px-Triode_tube_diagram.svg.png)

### Key Early Vacuum Tube Machines:
* **Colossus Mark I (1943):** Designed by Tommy Flowers at Bletchley Park to decrypt Nazi communications. The world's first **programmable electronic computer** (programmed manually using plugboards and cables).
* **ENIAC (1946):** Built by John Mauchly and J. Presper Eckert at the University of Pennsylvania. The world's first **general-purpose programmable electronic computer**. It could do 5,000 additions per second.

## 3. The Transistor Era & Solid-State Electronics

By the 1950s, vacuum tubes hit physical limits due to high power consumption, heat, and burnout frequency.

In 1947, Bell Labs scientists (John Bardeen, Walter Brattain, and William Shockley) invented the **transistor**.

* **How It Works:** Uses a **semiconductor** material to switch electrical current on or off via a gate electrode, removing the need for glass bulbs or vacuums.
* **Benefits:** 
  * **Solid-State:** No moving parts, no vacuum, highly durable.
  * **Speed & Size:** Early transistors switched 10,000 times per second and were significantly smaller than vacuum tubes.
  * **Cost & Efficiency:** Allowed for mass commercial production (e.g., the IBM 608 in 1957).

## 4. The Rise of Silicon Valley

Much of the pioneering development in semiconductors occurred in the Santa Clara Valley, California. Because silicon is the primary material used in semiconductors, the region earned its famous name: **Silicon Valley**.

## Summary Comparison

| Technology Era | Core Mechanism | Switching Speed | Major Disadvantage / Limitation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Electromechanical** | Mechanical Relays | ~50 times / sec | Mechanical wear, slow speed, physical bugs. |
| **Vacuum Tubes** | Thermionic Valves (Triodes) | ~1,000s times / sec | Fragile, high heat, burnouts. |
| **Transistors** | Solid-State Semiconductors | ~10,000+ times / sec (Millions today) | Complex quantum physics manufacturing (Resolved over time). |
